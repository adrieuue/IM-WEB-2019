PROTESTA

“On September 23, 1972, President Ferdinand Marcos declared that the country would be placed under Martial Law … It served as an attempt by the Marcos government to quell resistance and prevent the spread of radical and progressive thoughts. Control over print and broadcast media limited the public’s access to information and ideas.”
— Bea Saban

“Experimentation with new forms for protest songs began right after the imposition of Martial Law. However, because of the uncertainty of the times, these were not publicly performed until the late 1970s when more and more songwriters became con-
vinced of the urgent need to offer the public alternative songs to the already well-entrenched Pinoy pop music.”
— Teresita Gimenez-Maceda

MAY PANAHON

[THERE IS TIME]

Bong Ramilo and Rene Agbayani

“This song is entitled Awit ng Peti B. That’s what the people were familiar with. But the real title is May Panahon. It is about the pro-
cess of the awakening of the people, or let’s say, the activists, that decided to take action and fight the oppression and cruelty of the ruling Marcos.”
— Chickoy Pura (translated)

BABAE

[WOMAN]

Inang Laya

“This is one of the songs that were always sung at the rallies leading to the EDSA revolution. And to me, this song is important because it’s about the appreciation and empowerment of women, who I think are equal to men in the role of running society.”
— Cooky Chua (translated)

OY UTOL, BUTO'T BALAT KA NA'Y NATUTULOG KA PA

[HEY BROTHER, YOU'RE ALL FLESH AND BONES AND STILL YOU'RE ASLEEP]

Heber Bartolome

“The song decries the passivity prevalent among Filipinos [at the time] and chides the populace for not doing anything about their situation of unfreedom, inequality, and suffering. But the song does not end on this note. The cry of pain in the beginning becomes a shout of protest in the end.”
— Teresita Gimenez-Maceda

MUTYA

[BELOVED]

Aloysius Baes, after Bonifacio Abdon

“Arrested upon the declaration of martial law, [Aloysius Baes] endured prison through music, sometimes written on [his younger brother] Jonas’ music sheets, which the latter always carried with him on family visits to the stockade. One song that he managed to smuggle out of prison was a kundiman adapted from an original by Bonifacio Abdon, a musician of the Philippine Revolution and one of the early music teachers at the UP Conservatory of Music. It was one of the first underground protest songs against martial law, Mutya. ”
— Jo. Florendo B. Lontoc

HALINA

[COME]

Jess Santiago

“ Halina particularizes the basic issues of feudalism, capitalism and imperialism … On the whole, the songs of Jess Santiago serve as
the social conscience of the times. Each narration reveals an aspect of life under Martial law that will never find its way to government-controlled-media. The songs are eloquent testimonies of oppression people are made to endure.”
— Teresita Gimenez-Maceda

BALITA

[NEWS]

Asin

“ Balita was originally written to help address the conflicts going on in Mindanao during the 1970s, since the band members hail from Cebu. However, as the band’s popularity grew, their songs began to travel. When Balita reached the streets of Manila, people immediately used it as an anthem against the tyrannical regime they were experiencing during Martial Law”.
— Rafael Reynante

ALING PAG-IBIG PA

[WHICH OTHER LOVE]

Pat Castillo

“The songs of protest at the time of the Martial Law … usually weren’t direct attacks to the dictatorship. Usually, they were songs that
just state a love for the country, a desire for freedom, and a search for people’s rights.”
— Al Dimalanta (translated)
